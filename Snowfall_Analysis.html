Analyzing Houghton snowfall over the years¶

The Houghton snowfall records are maintained by Michigan Tech. The dataset contains 126 years of snowfall records from the year 1890 till 2017. Snowfall is measured near Houghton county memorial airport by Keweenaw Research Center operated by Michigan Tech.

Understanding the data¶

  • The given dataset is a temporal dataset containing 10 attributes, 9 of which are quantitative attributes (or metrics) and has one categorical attribute with sequential ordering.
  • The dataset given is a time-series dataset as we have the dataset in the format of (t,x) where t = time attribute and x=values.
  • The snowfall data was collected for the months October, November, December, January, February, March, April, May which can be further classified as seasons.

October, November - Fall December, January, February - Winter March, April, May - Spring

Data pre-processing¶

The given data needs to processed for better analysis. Some of the pre-processing task performed are:

  • Rename headers such as "Winter" to "Year" and also the month headers were replaced with full month name, Ex: January rather than jan.
  • The year attribute renamed above was of the form YYYY-(YYYY+1) starting from month October to May next year. This was rearranged to the format of YYYY. For example: the first item of the dataset had the year 1890-1891 starting from year 1890 October to 1891 May which was split into 2 items such as 1890 and 1891. The unavailable data for months [January to May] for the year 1890 was replaced with zero (assuming no snowfall for analysis purposes).
  • Changed the data types of the columns to float to have consistency across the dataset.

1.How does the overall trend of Houghton Snowfall looks like? How does the forecast look for next 22 years?¶

The trend of total snowfall (in inches) appears to be increasing over the years. There are a few interesting points:

  • We can see that from 1890 to 1920, the snowfall gradually decreases and from 1920 to 1992 we see an increasing trend of the amount of snowfall after which interestingly the snowfall amount starts to reduce again until current date.
  • The maximum and minimum snowfall observed are during 1978 with a record snowfall of 355.9 inches and during 1930 with a mere 81.29 inches of snow.

Here I have considered using both linear fit and a polynomial fit to find the amount of snowfall over the years in Houghton. We can see that using a linear curve fitting shows an increasing trend while using a polynomial curve fitting indicates a decreasing trend from the year 2000 till date.

The snowfall distribution over the years is not linear, hence using a polynomial of degree 3 clearly captures structure of the data.

Forecast:

  • Here we have forecasted using both linear regression and polynomial regression. Even though the linear regression appears appealing it fails to fit the snowfall distribution whereas a polynomial regression clearly fits the data better but is overfitting the dataset. In order to better generalize the curve fitting we can resort to polynomial of degree 2 which better generalizes the curve fit. Based on this, the forecast appears to have a gradual increase initially until 2009 and starts to gradually decrease going ahead.

3. How does the overall distribution look like? Does amount of snowfall insome months affect/influence others?¶

Parallel coordinate help us visualize the high dimensional dataset such as our current dataset. Also they provide the big picture of the distribution and relationship between the datasets.

  • We can also infer that the highest snowfall was recorded in december.
  • From the plot it is evident that the month of december shows huge variation in the amount of snowfall over the years. Similarly the month of January also shows high variation.
  • Whereas the month of october, april and may show very less variation. This behavior is also evidently seen and depicted in moving average plots.
  • With the help of parallel coordinates, we can also recognize outliers or anomalies. But parallel coordinates plots are not very accurate in doing so and can be used as a crude visual check on outliers. We can see two outliers clearly from the plot below.
  • The main question that parallel coordinates is to find the relationship between multiple attributes. One of the downsides is that we can only compare adjacent dimensions, However this is overcome by moving the dimensions.
  • However in the plot below, we do not see any significant correlations between the attributes. This is also evident from the correlation matrix computed using pearson method.

4. How is the trend of snowfall for each month over the years?¶

We use Simple Moving Average(SMA) for computing the moving averages over an window of 30 years. The main purpose of using moving averages is to smooth out any short term fluctuations in the data, this inturn allows us to visually more clearly the underlying trend in the dataset at hand.

From the below moving average plots, we can infer the following:

  • We can clearly see that, snowfall during the months of October, April and May have nearly remained constant over the century with very few noticible fluctuations.
  • The months November, February and March remain nearly constant until 1908 and show a gradually increasing snowfall trend until 1964, then start to decrease very gradually.
  • It is highly evident that the months of January and December have very high snowfall and contribute the most to the total snowfall every year.
  • The months December and January had a nearly constant snowfall until 1908 and shows drastic increase in the amount of snowfall until 1975, after which surprisingly the amount of snowfall starts decreasing.

def sliding_mean(data_array, window=30):
    slid_mean = []
    for i in range(0,len(data_array)-window):
        arr = np.array(data_array)
        slid_mean.append(np.sum(arr[i:(i+window)])/window)
    return slid_mean

5. What is the average snowfall per month? How is the snowfall distributed by month? Are there any abnormal snowfall records (Outliers)?¶

These questions can be better answered with a box plot which clearly indicates the five summary statistics such as minimum, first quartile,median, mean, third quartile which in our context indicates the average snowfall, spread of the amount of snowfall from the average for each month.

  • We can see that most of the snowfall amount in the month of october, november, december, January, February, March, April, May lie below 3, 29.6, 58.75,66.6,39.55,25.5,11,1 inches respectively.
  • Box plot clearly indicates the distribution of snowfall as we progress from fall months to winter and then summer.
  • We do not see any skewness in the snowfall in any of the months.
  • Also another important aspect of the box plot is the detection of outliers. In the parallel coordinate plots we were able to identify only two possible outliers, whereas in the box plot we can see the outliers more prominantly.
  • The outliers showcase a pattern in which the months where we observe heavy snowfall dont have many outliers as compared to months where the snowfall is below the average snowfall. This is due to relative high snowfall during FALL and SUMMER months.

6. Are there any grouping patterns in the snowfall dataset?¶

This can be answered using a Cluster map which is a juxtaposed version of a dendogram and heatmap plots.

  • A dendrogram is a tree diagram frequently used to illustrate the arrangement of the clusters produced by aglomerative hierarchical clustering.
  • A heat map is a graphical representation of data where the individual values contained in a matrix are represented as colors.

Using a cluster map we can clearly see the amount of snowfall over the years at a glance.

  • Also we can see which years have had similar amount of snowfall.
  • The clustermap also indicates which months have similar snowfall.
  • By looking at the clustermap below and the column dendogram, we see a clear distinction of months into 3 clusters (naming it as Low Snowfall, Moderate snowfall and High Snowfall) ordered by intensity of the snowfall.
